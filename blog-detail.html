The Complete Guide
to Mixing Low End:
Bass, Kick & Sub

A
Aryan Mehta
Head Instructor
May 14, 2025
8 min read
4.2k views
Share

Low end is the heartbeat of a mix. Get it right and your track sounds massive, club-ready, and powerful. Get it wrong and every listener — consciously or not — feels something is off. In this guide, we'll walk through the three pillars of a perfect low end: sidechain compression, frequency carving, and mono compatibility.

Why Low End Is the Hardest Part of Mixing

Most home studio monitors don't reproduce frequencies below 60 Hz accurately. This means a huge portion of your mix — the sub bass and the fundamental of your kick — is essentially invisible to you while you work. You can't hear it properly, yet it affects how your mix translates on club systems, earbuds, and streaming platforms.

Professional mix engineers solve this in three ways: using a spectrum analyser alongside their ears, checking mixes on multiple speaker systems, and — most importantly — understanding the rules of the frequency spectrum so well that they can make correct decisions even without perfect monitoring.

Key Takeaway
  • Sub bass lives from 20–60 Hz. Keep it mono, keep it clean.
  • The kick drum's body punch sits around 60–120 Hz.
  • Bass guitar and synth bass fundamentals dominate 80–200 Hz.
  • Low-mid mud accumulates between 200–350 Hz — clean it up.

Sidechain Compression: Making Kick and Bass Work Together

The most common low-end problem in modern music is the kick and bass fighting each other. Both instruments have energy in the same frequency range (80–120 Hz), and when they both hit at the same time, you get a muddy, undefined collision.

The solution is sidechain compression — routing the kick drum's signal to trigger a compressor placed on the bass track. Every time the kick hits, the compressor briefly ducks the bass volume by a few decibels, creating space for the kick to punch through. The result is that clean, bouncy "pumping" energy you hear in Afrobeats, EDM, and hip-hop.

"The kick and bass should dance together, not fight. Sidechain gives them room to breathe."
— Aryan Mehta, Avotune Studio

Recommended Sidechain Settings to Start With

  • Attack: 2–5ms (fast enough to catch the transient)
  • Release: 50–120ms (let the bass breathe back naturally)
  • Ratio: 4:1 to 8:1 (enough duck without sounding obvious)
  • Threshold: Set so the GR meter shows 3–6 dB of reduction

Frequency Carving: EQ for a Clean Low End

Even with sidechain dialled in, you'll still have frequency overlap. This is where surgical EQ comes in. The goal is to carve a defined space for each element so they're complementary, not competitive. 

KICK DRUM EQ APPROACH
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Add    : +3 dB @ 60–80 Hz   → Thump / sub body
Add    : +2 dB @ 3–5 kHz    → Transient click
Cut    : -4 dB @ 200–350 Hz → Reduce boxiness
High-pass: 30 Hz            → Remove inaudible sub clutter

BASS / 808 EQ APPROACH
─────────────────────────────────────────────
Add    : +2 dB @ 80–120 Hz  → Fundamental warmth
Cut    : -3 dB @ 60 Hz      → Leave this for the kick
Cut    : -4 dB @ 250–400 Hz → Clean up mud
High-pass: 40 Hz            → Remove sub rumble

Mono Compatibility: The Most Overlooked Step

Here's a fact that surprises many new producers: most club speakers, phone speakers, and mono Bluetooth devices collapse stereo information below ~120 Hz to mono. This means if your bass is wide and stereo, it will actually cancel itself out on these systems — leaving you with almost no low end where it matters most.

The rule is simple: keep everything below 120 Hz in mono. In most DAWs, you can do this with a mid/side EQ or a dedicated mono-bass plugin — cut the side channel (stereo information) on your bass and kick tracks below 120 Hz.

Common Mistake

Using stereo chorus or stereo wideners on your bass synth without cutting the low-frequency stereo content. This sounds amazing in your headphones but destroys your low end on mono systems.

The 3-System Check

Before calling a mix done, always check your low end on at least three different playback systems:

  1. Studio monitors (or whatever you mix on)
  2. Phone or laptop speaker (small, harsh, mono)
  3. Earphones / AirPods (how most people will actually hear it)

If the kick still punches and the bass still grooves on all three — your low end is done. If the bass disappears on your phone speaker, you have a mono compatibility problem. If the kick sounds boomy and undefined on your monitors, go back to your EQ.

Low end mastery takes time and repetition, but it is absolutely a learnable skill. At Avotune Studio, every student mixes on our calibrated KRK Rokit 8 monitors paired with a subwoofer — so you can hear exactly what you're doing from day one.
